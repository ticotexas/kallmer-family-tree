# Floyd Kallmer and Habitat for Humanity

## Doing His Part

By the spring of 2000, Floyd Kallmer was 93 years old, long retired, and just over a year removed from selling the house at 427 Geneseo Street where he and two wives had raised a family and hosted the better part of a century's worth of Storm Lake gatherings. He and Dorcas were settled into independent living at Methodist Manor. He had, by any reasonable measure, earned the right to simply rest.

Instead, the *Storm Lake Times* found him doing something else entirely — writing a check to Habitat for Humanity. The paper ran a short item on April 1, 2000, under a headline that could double as a description of the man himself:

> "Kallmer donates to Habitat cause. Floyd Kallmer is doing his part to build a better world. Kallmer recently joined donors and volunteers who are... Now retired, Kallmer owned an appliance store called Kallmer Sales and is a long-time resident of Storm Lake. Kallmer is pleased with the good things Habitat [is doing]..."

## A Retailer's Instinct for Giving Back

It is a small item — a few sentences, easy to miss in a community paper — but it fits the pattern of a man whose whole working life had been built around the motto "We Service the Products We Sell." Floyd had spent 34 years making sure Storm Lake families had working refrigerators and reliable televisions. In retirement, with the store long since sold and his own name attached to nothing but his history in the town, he turned that same instinct toward a different kind of home-building: helping make sure other Storm Lake families had a roof over their heads at all.

## Nine Months Later

The timing carries its own quiet weight. Floyd died on January 30, 2001 — just nine months after the Habitat item ran. It stands as one of the last public acts of his life recorded in the newspaper archive: a 93-year-old man, decades removed from his own storefront, still finding a way to be useful to his town right up to the end. Dorcas would later carry that same instinct further still, making the gift a few years later that created the Kallmer Education Center at Buena Vista Regional Medical Center — but Floyd's small, unassuming Habitat donation in the spring of 2000 was very much his own.
