# Obituary of Floyd F. "Stub" Kallmer

Funeral services for Floyd F. Kallmer were held Friday, Feb. 2, 2001 at St. Mary's Catholic Church in Storm Lake with Father Robert J. Schimmer officiating. Prayer was given by Rev. Duane Queen.

Music was provided by St. Mary's Choir and Jeanette Hinkeldey, organist. Maxine Zubradt and Maxine Lenz were in charge of flowers. Honorary attendants were Bill Roggow, Dr. Robert Mailliard, Bill Johnson, Claude Thompson, Rollin Sievers and Jerry Seiler. Active attendants were Tim Kallmer, Josh Kallmer, Brent Kallmer, Brent Humburg, Jim Faust and Bob Lovstad.

Burial was in St. Mary's Cemetery. Farber and Otteman Funeral Home was in charge of arrangements.

## Life and Legacy

Floyd “Stub” Kallmer died at noon on Tuesday, Jan. 30, 2001, weighed down by his 94 years and five days.

He started out none too auspiciously in losing his mother to tuberculosis in his first year. He was raised by grandparents, Aunt Kate and eldest sister, Isabelle at Odebolt, where he engaged in such exploits as flying his kite from the top of the water tower and “feeling for fish” in the Boyer River — catching them with his bare hands.

He played football in high school and it was a young lawyer from Ida Grove, Zene White, who, having seen him play, got him an athletic scholarship to Buena Vista College. Otherwise he could not have afforded it, $65 a semester tuition and $10 lab fee. However, he did afford a car, one of three on campus in the years 1927-31, parked all three in a row outside Old Main on College Street. One was the college president's, another a professor's, and his. This was a necessity — to transport him around the counties to several ballroom dances a week.

Getting up in the middle of the night to work in his father's Storm Lake Bakery couldn't have left much time for sleep. So the young Stub led an intense, though carefree, existence while quarterbacking the Beavers, and on graduation in 1933 turned down a job offer of $5 a week to work for Toy State Bank in Sioux City and became a traveling representative for Firestone instead. He quit when manager of their Corpus Christi, Tx., store to return to Storm Lake and start Kallmer Sales Co., his dealership in home appliances for 35 years. He took his turn as president of the Chamber of Commerce and Exalted Ruler of the Elks Lodge, was a member of the country club and a devoted parishioner of St. Mary's.

A widower at 50, his dog Koko fell in love with Dorcas first, as he liked to tell it. Koko would turn up at Dorcas's door and Stub would have to drive over and get him. Bad dog! Oh, yes.

## Predeceased and Surviving Family

Almost all his friends and associates have predeceased him, including Z.Z. White, Dr. Howard Pierce, the fearsome foursome of Al Faust, Wayne Flohr, Elmer Knebel, plus Sid Hyllested, Milt Rystad, Eino Kiiponen, Charlie Zalesky and many others.

To the end he was the quarterback, the businessman, the parishioner of St. Mary's, hunter, fisherman and golfer, traveler to Rome, Alaska and Mexico, father to Robert, James and William, husband to Edith and Dorcas, the love of his life. And James settled in Seattle and took Diana to wife and beget Josh and Brent, and William wed LaDon and their children are Timothy of Austin, Tx., married to Sunny, and Cherlyn of Omaha, married to Brent Humburg.

Thus passes a life that spanned nearly a century and thus departs a resident of Storm Lake for nearly half of its history.
